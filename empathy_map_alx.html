Empathy Map
Career Readiness

Sadat Academy · Cairo, Egypt · 2024–2025

01

Who Are We Empathizing With?

🎓
The Underprepared Undergraduate

A full-time undergraduate student (years 2–4) at Sadat Academy for Management Sciences, aged 19–23, from a middle-income Egyptian family in Greater Cairo. Academically engaged but professionally invisible — no internship, no employer contacts, no CV beyond a transcript. Approaching graduation with growing anxiety and no institutional support system to prepare them for the job market.

Age 19–23 Middle-income Greater Cairo No work experience Digitally active Arabic primary Family-dependent finances Theory-heavy curriculum No professional network High academic pressure
02

What Do They Need To Do?

Job to Get Done

Graduate career-ready with practical skills and employer connections by June 2025

Do Differently

Move beyond passive studying — actively seek internships, build a CV, engage with professionals

Decision to Make

Which career path to pursue and how to compete against graduates from better-resourced universities

Success Looks Like

Securing at least one internship before graduation and a job offer within 6 months of graduating

03–06

See · Say · Do · Hear

👁
See 03
  • Private university peers attending career fairs and landing internships with no equivalent available to them
  • Job listings demanding 1–2 years experience for "entry-level" roles
  • No career office, no internship board, no visible alumni network on campus
  • Professors teaching from theory-heavy textbooks disconnected from market realities
  • Family advising "just get the degree" with no guidance on what comes after
  • Social media content showing peers building LinkedIn profiles and personal brands
  • News reporting high youth unemployment and economic pressure in Egypt
💬
Say 04
  • "I don't even know where to start looking for an internship."
  • "My CV is basically empty — I have nothing to show employers."
  • "Why don't companies come to recruit at our university like they do elsewhere?"
  • "I use LinkedIn but I don't know how to make it work for me."
  • "I want to work in [field] but I've never spoken to anyone who actually does that job."
  • "Professors are great academically, but they can't tell me how to get a job."
  • "I feel like we graduate and then we're on our own."
⚡
Do 05
  • Study for exams — academic performance is their primary focus and identity
  • Search for opportunities through personal connections (wasta) rather than formal channels
  • Spend significant time on social media but rarely for professional development
  • Occasionally take free online courses (Coursera, YouTube) without structure or direction
  • Rely on friends and cousins to share job leads second-hand
  • Avoid asking professors for career help — not seen as part of the academic relationship
  • Take informal, unrelated part-time work to support family income
🎧
Hear 06
  • From family: "A good degree will open doors" — advice no longer matching market reality
  • From peers: "Internships at good companies go to AUC/BUE students, not us"
  • From social media: influencers promoting hustle culture, LinkedIn growth, personal branding
  • From news: reports on high youth unemployment and economic difficulty in Egypt
  • From employers (second-hand): "Fresh graduates lack soft skills and real-world experience"
  • From older graduates: months of job applications with no response due to no experience
  • From faculty: "Try to find an internship over summer" — with zero structured support
07

What Do They Think & Feel?

🧠

Pains & Gains — The Inner World

😰 Pains
  • Deep anxiety about graduating with no job in a market where youth unemployment exceeds 18%
  • Fear of being permanently behind peers from better-resourced universities
  • Frustration that four years of academic effort may not translate into professional opportunity
  • Shame around an empty CV and uncertainty about where to even begin
  • Fear of being a financial burden on family if unable to find work after graduation
  • Feeling invisible and underestimated by employers who associate quality with private university names
  • For female students: additional anxiety about navigating workplaces with no visible female role models
  • Sense that the skills gap between what they learned and what employers want is too wide to close alone
🌱 Gains
  • A clear, structured pathway — concrete steps to become employable, not just inspiration
  • A mentor already working in their field who can show them a realistic career trajectory
  • Practical experience to point to on a CV: an internship, a project, a certification
  • An employer willing to give them a first chance and see their potential
  • Financial independence and the ability to support their family
  • Recognition that their Sadat Academy degree carries real professional credibility
  • A professional identity — knowing who they are in the market and how to communicate it
  • The sense that their institution is genuinely invested in their future, not just their tuition
